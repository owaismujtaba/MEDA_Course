Based on the PCA Score Plot (pca_nature), the structural difference between India's trade with China and the Rest of the World (ROW) is visually undeniable. The plot reveals two perfectly separated clusters along the principal axes: the "China Class" and the "ROW Class." There is absolutely no overlap between these two groups across the 20-year period, which mathematically confirms that the "chemical signature" (the mix of HS chapters) of imports from China is fundamentally unique. While both clusters show a trajectory over time (moving along PC1, which likely represents trade growth), they remain isolated on opposite ends of the structural axis (PC2), proving that China serves a specialized and non-interchangeable role in India's industrial supply chain that the rest of the world has not filled.

In the context of India’s derisking strategy, these PCA loadings reveal that the "structural DNA" of India's dependence on China is rooted in specialized manufacturing inputs like Knitted Fabrics (Ch 60), Headgear (Ch 65), and Toys (Ch 95), rather than just high-volume commodities. The extreme vertical separation in the score plot, driven by these specific chapters, indicates a significant "substitution gap": the Rest of the World currently lacks the industrial architecture to replace China in these categories. For India, this means that "derisking" cannot be a simple matter of shifting partners; it requires a targeted industrial policy to build domestic capacity in these precise "marker" sectors, as they represent the areas where India’s supply chain is most uniquely tied to Chinese production and most vulnerable to disruption.
